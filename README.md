📖 Chapter 1: Ten Principles of EconomicsSource: Principles of Economics, Ninth Edition by N. Gregory Mankiw The word economy originates from the Greek word oikonomos, meaning "one who manages a household". Much like a household, a society must make numerous decisions regarding the allocation of its scarce resources based on ability, effort, and desire.🔍 Core DefinitionsScarcity: The limited nature of society’s resources; society cannot produce all the goods and services people wish to have.Economics: The study of how society manages its scarce resources.I. How People Make Decisions 1. People Face Trade-offs"There ain't no such thing as a free lunch". To get something we like, we usually have to give up something else that we also like.Efficiency: Society getting the maximum benefits from its scarce resources (the size of the economic pie).Equality: Distributing economic prosperity uniformly among members of society (how the pie is divided).2. The Cost of Something Is What You Give Up to Get ItMaking decisions requires comparing the costs and benefits of alternatives.Opportunity Cost: Whatever must be given up to obtain some item.3. Rational People Think at the MarginRational people systematically and purposefully do the best they can to achieve their objectives.Marginal Changes: Small incremental adjustments to a plan of action.Decision Rule: A rational decision-maker takes action only if the Marginal Benefit > Marginal Cost.4. People Respond to IncentivesAn incentive is something that induces a person to act.Market Effect: Higher prices cause buyers to consume less and sellers to produce more.Policy Caution: Policies can have unintended consequences, such as seat belt laws potentially increasing pedestrian deaths by encouraging faster driving.II. How People Interact 5. Trade Can Make Everyone Better OffTrade allows each person to specialize in the activities he or she does best, resulting in a greater variety of goods and services.6. Markets Are Usually a Good Way to Organize Economic ActivityIn a market economy, resource allocation occurs through the decentralized decisions of many firms and households as they interact.The Invisible Hand: Adam Smith's concept that households and firms act as if guided by an "invisible hand" to desirable market outcomes.7. Governments Can Sometimes Improve Market OutcomesGovernments are needed to enforce property rights so individuals can own and control scarce resources.Market Failure: When a market fails to allocate resources efficiently, often due to Externalities (e.g., pollution) or Market Power.III. How the Economy as a Whole Works 8. A Country’s Standard of Living Depends on Its ProductivityLarge differences in living standards across nations are explained by productivity.Productivity: The quantity of goods and services produced from each unit of labor input.9. Prices Rise When the Government Prints Too Much MoneyInflation is an increase in the overall level of prices in the economy. It is primarily caused by growth in the quantity of money, which causes its value to fall.10. Society Faces a Short-Run Trade-off Between Inflation and UnemploymentOver a period of a year or two, many economic policies push inflation and unemployment in opposite directions. This trade-off plays a key role in the analysis of the business cycle.
